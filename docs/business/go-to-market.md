# Go-to-Market Strategy

> **Last updated:** 2026-03-03
> **Status:** Draft

## Core Insight: Create the Category Before Selling Into It

Project Zeus is a new category for most Australian universities. They don't have a line
item for "marketing targeting intelligence" in their budgets, and they won't issue
procurement tenders for a product they don't know exists.

This means formal procurement channels (VendorPanel, AusTender, university tender
portals) are the **wrong starting point**. These platforms are designed for known,
scoped needs — media buying agencies, IT infrastructure, facilities management — where
universities already know what they want to buy.

Our job in Phase 1 is to **demonstrate value before anyone asks for it**, using our
unique advantage: we can generate a complete marketing intelligence brief for any
Australian university without their cooperation or even their knowledge.

---

## Why Not Tenders (Yet)

| Factor | Why it's a problem |
|---|---|
| **Tenders require a defined need** | Universities won't tender for "market intelligence platform" — they don't know the category exists |
| **Procurement cycles are slow** | 6-12 months from RFP to signed contract; we need validation sooner |
| **Tenders favour incumbents** | Require insurance, ABN history, references, and case studies that early-stage products lack |
| **Wrong category** | University tenders on platforms like VendorPanel are predominantly operational (facilities, IT, construction, consulting) |

Tenders become relevant **after** we've established the category — once 2-3 universities
are paying customers, others will start writing RFPs that describe what we do. That's
when procurement portals become a channel, not a barrier.

## Sales Channels (Ranked by Expected ROI)

### Tier 1: Direct Outreach to Marketing Directors

**Why this is #1:** Australian university marketing teams are small (5-20 people). The
decision-maker is usually a **Director of Marketing**, **Director of Student
Recruitment**, or **Chief Marketing Officer**. These people are identifiable, reachable,
and under acute pressure to justify marketing spend.

**The play: Unsolicited university briefs**

Our strongest sales asset is the ability to generate a personalised marketing
intelligence brief for any university using only public data. The approach:

1. **Generate a brief** for the target university (see
   [UOW example](example-brief-uow.md)) — brand gap analysis, program prioritisation,
   audience segments, geographic targeting, and campaign recommendations
2. **Send it cold** to the marketing director with a short note: "Here's what the public
   data says about your competitive position. Happy to walk you through it."
3. **Book a 30-minute walkthrough** — show them their own data in the interactive
   dashboards. This is the "instant demo" advantage described in our
   [integration strategy](integration-strategy.md).

This works because:
- It's impossible to ignore intelligence about your own institution
- It demonstrates the product's value before any commercial conversation
- It requires zero effort from the prospect (no data sharing, no IT review, no onboarding)
- It positions us as already knowing their market — not pitching a generic tool

**Target profile:**
- Mid-tier and regional universities (not Group of Eight, who may have internal capability)
- Institutions under financial pressure (Federation University, University of Canberra,
  CQUniversity, Southern Cross, Western Sydney)
- Universities with recent leadership changes in marketing (new CMO = new initiatives)

**Where to find them:**
- LinkedIn (primary channel — this is where university marketers live professionally)
- University staff directories (marketing/communications department pages)
- ATEM and EPHEA membership directories

### Tier 2: Conference Presence

**Target events:**
- **ATEM** (Association for Tertiary Education Management) — national and state conferences
- **EPHEA** (Equity Practitioners in Higher Education Australasia)
- **Universities Australia** — annual conference
- **CAUDIT** (Council of Australasian University Directors of IT) — for data/analytics angle
- **ContentEd** — higher education digital content conference

**The play:**
- Submit a talk proposal: *"What 8 government datasets tell us about your competitive
  position"* — positions us as domain experts, not vendors
- Offer a free personalised mini-brief to anyone who visits our stand or attends the talk
- Collect contacts for Tier 1 follow-up

Conferences are Tier 2 (not Tier 1) because they're seasonal, expensive, and broadcast
rather than targeted. But they build credibility and generate warm leads for direct
outreach.

### Tier 3: Agency Partnerships

Many universities outsource digital marketing to specialist agencies. If we position Zeus
as the **intelligence layer that feeds their campaign strategy**, agencies become a
distribution channel.

**How it works:**
- Agency uses Zeus insights to inform campaign targeting for their university clients
- Agency bundles Zeus data into their retainer or strategy fees
- We get distribution without direct university sales; agency gets differentiated
  competitive positioning

**Target agencies:**
- Higher education specialist agencies (search for agencies listing universities as clients)
- Media buying agencies with university accounts
- Student recruitment consultancies

**Risks:**
- Agencies may resist sharing the intelligence source (want to appear as the smart ones)
- Lower margin per university (agency takes a cut)
- Less direct relationship with the end customer

Agency partnerships are Tier 3 because they take longer to establish and involve more
relationship management. But they can scale faster than direct sales once proven.

### Tier 4: Tertiary Admissions Centres (TACs)

UAC, QTAC, VTAC, SATAC, and TISC collectively process applications for almost every
Australian university. They already have relationships with all institutions in their
state and provide data services.

**The play:**
- Partner with a TAC to offer Zeus insights as a value-add alongside their existing
  application data
- TACs benefit from a richer product offering; we benefit from their established trust
  and distribution

This is Tier 4 because TACs are large, slow-moving organisations with complex governance.
But they represent the ultimate distribution channel if the product proves out.

## Pricing Strategy: Get Under the Tender Threshold

Most Australian universities can approve purchases under **$25,000-$50,000** without
going through a formal procurement or tender process (the exact threshold varies by
institution). This is critical for Phase 1.

**Phase 1 pricing should be designed to stay under this threshold:**

| Offering | Indicative Price | What's Included |
|---|---|---|
| **One-off university brief** | $5,000-$10,000 | Personalised marketing intelligence report with campaign recommendations |
| **Pilot subscription** (6 months) | $15,000-$25,000 | Dashboard access + quarterly brief updates + one strategy session |
| **Annual subscription** | $30,000-$50,000 | Full dashboard access + monthly updates + dedicated support |

The one-off brief serves double duty: it's both a sales tool (sent free to open doors)
and a paid product (once the prospect sees value and wants ongoing updates or deeper
analysis).

Once a university is a paying customer, expansion within the institution (additional
departments, additional report modules) can grow the account above the tender threshold
— but by then we have an internal champion and proven value.

## Phase 1 Execution Plan

### Target List: First 10 Universities

Select 10 universities based on:
1. **Financial pressure** — publicly reported budget cuts, deficits, or staff reductions
2. **Mid-tier positioning** — not Group of Eight (internal capability), not too small (no budget)
3. **Geographic spread** — demonstrate the product works across states
4. **Data availability** — we need UAC/QTAC/VTAC data for their state to generate a strong brief

### Actions

1. **Generate briefs** for each target university (automated from existing pipeline +
   manual narrative layer)
2. **Identify the decision-maker** at each institution via LinkedIn and staff directories
3. **Send personalised outreach** — email with attached brief and offer to walk through
4. **Follow up** within 1 week if no response — reference a specific finding from their
   brief
5. **Convert walkthroughs to pilots** — propose a 6-month pilot under the procurement
   threshold

### Success Metrics

| Metric | Target |
|---|---|
| Briefs generated | 10 |
| Walkthroughs booked | 3-5 |
| Pilot agreements signed | 1-2 |
| Timeline | 3 months from first outreach |

## Phase 2: Productise and Expand

Once 2-3 pilots validate willingness to pay:
- Package the product into self-service dashboard tiers
- Develop case studies from pilot universities (with permission)
- Begin conference circuit with proven results
- Explore agency partnerships with pilot evidence
- Consider whether formal procurement registration (VendorPanel, SAP Ariba) is
  justified by inbound demand

## Phase 3: Scale and Formalise

With 5-10 customers and case studies:
- Register on university procurement platforms (now we have the references they require)
- Hire a dedicated sales/account manager
- Expand data coverage to additional states (QTAC, VTAC, SATAC, TISC)
- Consider international expansion (UK UCAS data, Canadian equivalents)
- Evaluate agency white-label model at scale

## Key Assumptions and Risks

| Assumption | Risk if Wrong | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| Marketing directors will read an unsolicited brief | Low open rates; briefs ignored | Personalise heavily; reference specific institutional challenges; use LinkedIn warm-up before email |
| Universities can approve $15-25K without tender | Some institutions have lower thresholds or freeze on discretionary spend | Offer a free brief first; adjust pricing downward if needed |
| Mid-tier universities lack internal analytics capability | Some may have stronger data teams than expected | Pre-qualify by checking for analytics job postings and team structure on LinkedIn |
| Conference talks will be accepted | Competitive speaker slots; may not align with conference themes | Submit to multiple events; offer workshops as alternative to keynotes |
| Agencies will partner rather than replicate | Agencies could attempt to build similar analysis internally | Move fast; establish relationships before the approach is widely known; maintain data engineering moat |
